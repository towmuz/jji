Thrifty

Role
Concept, Research, Visuals
Duration
August to October 2022

Project Overview

Thrifty is a second-hand clothing store that offers a way for users to buy items from independent sellers, boutiques, and charity shops. The typical user is between 18 - 35 years old, and most users are college students or early career professionals. Thrifty’s goal is to make shopping for second-hand clothes fun, simple, and discoverable for all types of users.

The Problem

As the global pandemic continues to force thrift and charity stores to close and the rising interest in sustainable second-hand clothing options, it's created a need for a dedicated platform where people can shop for quality second-hand clothing products.

The Goal

Design a user-friendly responsive website empowering users to buy, sell and donate quality second-hand clothes and provide an easy-to-use navigation flow for browsing and discovering products.

The Design Process

Understanding The User

User Research

I conducted user interviews, which I then turned into empathy maps to better understand the target user and their needs. I discovered that many target users enjoy shopping at thrift stores to search for unique and stylish clothes. In addition, with wanting to have a positive environmental impact and save money. However, many thrift stores are not available online, and stores are often overwhelming, unorganized, and hard to navigate, frustrating many target users.

User Pain Points

Navigation

Users find thrift stores unorganized and hard to navigate, while browsing in-store.

Experience

Online shopping websites don’t provide an engaging browsing experience.

Discoverability

Users felt that consignment and thrift stores have poor product discoverability.

Product Condition

Users are apprehensive about the poor condition and durability of second-hand items.

User Persona - Matteo

Problem Statement

Matteo is a busy professional and fashion lover who needs a way to easily shop online for stylish and unique clothes because browsing for items in-store takes too much time and is often unorganized.

User Journey Map

I created a user journey map of Matteo’s online shopping experience to help identify possible pain points and improvement opportunities.

User Persona - Amaya

Problem Statement

Amaya is a busy student and employee who needs a way to shop online for affordable second-hand clothes and donate unwanted items because they want to save money and contribute towards a good cause.

User Journey Map

I created a user journey map of Amaya’s in-store shopping and donating experience to help identify possible pain points and improvement opportunities.

Starting The Design

Sitemap

Difficulty with navigation and unorganized products was a primary pain point for users, so I used that knowledge to create a sitemap. My goal was to make strategic information architecture decisions that would improve overall website navigation. The structure I chose was designed to make things simple and easy.

Screen Size Variations

Low-Fidelity Prototype

To create a low-fidelity prototype, I connected all the screens involved in the buyer's user flow of browsing and adding an item to the shopping bag, and checking out. In addition, I also created the seller's user flow of setting up a store and listing an item. At this point, I had received feedback on my designs from members of online design communities about the placement of buttons and page organization. I made sure to listen to their feedback, and I implemented several suggestions in places that addressed user pain points.

Usability Study

Study Type

Unmoderated usability study

Location

United States, remote

Participants

5 participants

Length

20-30 minutes

Findings

These were the main findings uncovered by the usability study:

Checkout

Users struggled to go back and editing their shipping address or payment information.

Personalized Homepage

User commented on how the homepage did not provide a personalized shopping experience.

Store Setup

Users found it annoying clicking through every section when setting up a store.

Listing an Item

Once at the list an item screen, users were confused with the page layout and some terminology.

Refining The Design

Mockups

Based on the insights from the usability study, I made changes to improve the site’s checkout flow. One of the changes I made was adding the option to go back and edit the shipping address and payment method. This allowed users more freedom to edit their order without going through a complicated process to change or remove information.

I refined the homepage and added a recently viewed items section to provide a more personalized shopping experience.

To make the store setup flow even easier for users who want to sell items, I combined the process into a single page that allowed users to open a store fast and easy.

Screen Size Variations

I included considerations for additional screen sizes in my mockups based on my earlier wireframes. Because users shop from a variety of devices, I felt it was important to optimize the browsing experience for a range of device sizes, such as mobile and tablet so users have the smoothest experience possible.

High-Fidelity Prototype

My hi-fi prototype followed the same user flow as the lo-fi prototype, and included the design changes made after the usability study, as well as several changes suggested by members of the online design community.

Accessibility Considerations

I used headings with different sized text for clear visual hierarchy.
‍

I used landmarks to help users navigate the site, including users who rely on assistive technologies.
‍

I designed the site with alt text available on each page for smooth screen reader access.

Going Forward

Takeaways

Impact

Our target users shared that the design was intuitive to navigate through, more engaging with the images, and demonstrated a clear visual hierarchy.
‍

What I Learned

While designing Thrifty, I learned that the first ideas for the app are only the beginning of the process. Conducting usability studies and peer feedback influenced each iteration of the app’s designs, improving the user experience. I also learned that even a small design change can have a huge impact on the user experience. The most important takeaway for me is to always focus on the real needs of the user when coming up with design ideas and solutions.

Next Steps

Perform another round of usability studies to validate whether the pain points users experienced have been effectively addressed and also to conduct more user research to determine any new areas of need. I would create more designs for the donation process and test the user flow with users to optimize the user experience. In addition to identifying any additional areas of need and ideating on new features.
