Honors Project: Analysis of Online News Websites for People with Dyslexia

Role
Concept, Research, Visuals
Duration
September 2022 to October 2022

Project Overview

The Edinburgh Times is an online daily news website based in Edinburgh, Scotland. They provide users with the latest news, sports, business, and more. The typical user is adults between 18-40 years old, and most users are local working professionals. The Edinburgh Times' goal is to make access to the news easy and accessible for all types of users.

The Problem

Available online news websites have cluttered designs, poor text presentation for legible reading, and inefficient website search systems.

The Goal

Design the Edinburgh Time’s website to be user-friendly and accessible to people with reading difficulties by providing an easy-to-use experience, empowering users to customize the presentation of the website's content and a straightforward layout.

Understanding The User

User Research

To understand the user and their needs, I conducted a usability study on several news websites. The study used a diverse set of qualitative and quantitative data collection techniques including, interviews, surveys, observation tasks, eye-tracking technology and, a tool to measure users' emotions.
‍

Usability Study Parameters

Study Type

Moderated Usability Study

Location

Edinburgh Napier University, in person at the usability lab.

Participants

7 dyslexic participants, four females and three males.

Length

40-60 minutes

Analyzing & Synthesizing The Results

I learned that intrusive advertisement banners disrupted users' experience, resulting in a loss of focus and engagement on the website's article. A search function that provides word suggestions would allow for an easier experience when searching the site. I observed that participants incorrectly spelled the term "Dyslexia" resulting in no articles displaying, leaving the participant confused. Participants also had trouble clicking links on the website because of the hover-sensitive navigation bar. While moving the mouse cursor, the submenu kept disappearing.

I discovered that medium to large news card elements was more engaging when compared to small news card elements. Also, users would benefit from an improved page hierarchy and whitespace. In addition, participants commented on how they felt overwhelmed and unsure where to look at content due to the visual complexity of the website. Some participants suggested that implementing larger-sized text, sans serif font, and user customization would benefit them. The ability to customize the background color would also help users who found it difficult to read online due to fuzziness or movement of words.

User Pain Points

Presentation of Text

Users found it challenging to read effectively due to the presentation of text on the page.

Visually Complex

The amount of information and elements on the page made users feel overwhelmed, making it difficult to focus on the relevant content.

Search Auto-Complete

Many news websites don't have auto-complete functionality, for users who have difficulty spelling.

Content Organization

The organization of page content is often inconsistent, splitting the relevant content with advertisements and irrelevant material, making it disjointed and confusing for users to read.

User Persona

Problem Statement

Eugene is a busy professional and father who needs intuitive navigation, adaptive reading experience, and auto-complete features because they want to read and discover news articles stress-free.

Starting The Design

Digital Wireframes

I started creating digital wireframes, which allowed me to outline the structure and layout of the website. I made sure to base the screen designs on feedback and findings from the user research. This is the first wireframe that demonstrates the design thinking aligned with user research. Prioritizing accessible features and the visual element placement on the home page was a key part of my strategy.

Providing users with the ability to customize the background color, text size, and typeface would help users who had difficulty reading online and were all key user needs to address in the design. In addition to allowing assistive technologies to work and users having news articles read aloud to them. The second wireframe demonstrates the design thinking aligned with user research.

Refining The Design

Mockups

I wanted to add more fidelity to the designs to help closely match the look and feel of the final product. When designing my mockups, I kept in mind the visual elements I wanted to use on each screen of the design, such as typography, color, and iconography.

As I continued to refine my designs, I kept the user's pain points in mind while adding words, images, icons, visual elements, and accessibility features on the home and article pages.

High-Fidelity Prototype

The final high-fidelity prototype followed the same user flow as the earlier designs and illustrates how users can easily search for articles by automatically suggesting the correct word or phrase if misspelled.

Users can customize the website's typeface, text size, and background color, enhancing their reading experience while using the site. Users can also use the news feed filter to view topics they are interested in, allowing them to navigate through the articles on the homepage.

I created the prototype using Adobe XD, and it consists of several variations of the news homepage, search results, and article pages.

Accessibility Considerations

Color palette is an acceptable contrast ratio based on the WCAG 2.0 standards and customizable foreground and background colors reduce visual stress and sensitivity when reading online.
‍

Users can have website articles read aloud to them, allowing them to read articles without difficulty.
‍

The website contains a less visually complex layout and improved elements for easier navigation.
‍

Provided website customization features to change text size and typeface, improving users' ability to focus and read legibly.

Going Forward

Takeaways

Impact

The target users shared that the design was clean and had a user-friendly interface. They also shared that the design was intuitive to navigate, more engaging, and allowed them the flexibility to customize their experience and tailor it to their needs.
‍

What I Learned

In this project I learned that even a small design change can have a huge impact on the user experience. It was a real eye opener on what more we can do for people with dyslexia and people with reading difficulties , but the project still needs some iteration as a project is never really finished and always needs some improvement. Overall the project provided an experience that is more flexible, not just for users with accessibility needs, such as dyslexia, but everyone.
‍

Quotes From User Feedback

I love the ability to change the background and text color contrast. This will assist me in reading online.
I genuinely like the news feed filter, as it will allow me to pinpoint what I'm looking for and provides an excellent way to funnel through particular topics on the homepage.
My favorite thing about this design is having articles read aloud to you. I often struggle to read sophisticated words and focus on long paragraphs. I love this!

Next Steps

To conduct another round of usability studies to validate whether the pain points users experienced have been effectively addressed. I also would want to conduct more user research to determine any new areas of need. In addition, I think adapting these improvements into a browser plug-in or software version would allow users to benefit from these improvements across multiple websites.
